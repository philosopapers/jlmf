Jacques Rancière
Substance 33 (1):10-24 (2004)
Abstract "Ham", to some, is simply a signifier for the cured, consumable, omnipresent flesh of the pig. To a Frenchman, it is a symbol of national pride, tinged with inter-European rivalries. Roland Barthes was the first to discuss the semiology of food in the creation of a natural character, however as he was the first, he did it with a notable brutality and crudity. To represent ham is to stand for something else (not ham), it is thus to lie about the truth of ham. The work of Esther Shalev-Gerz doubly refutes this presupposition : on the one hand, the ham itself is never there, there is only representation (of the ham): words borne by bodies, images (of ham) which present to us, not what words say but what these bodies do ; on the other hand, there is never any representation, one is always confronted with presence : hams, the hands that touch them (the ham), mouths that speak of them (also the ham), ears that listen (to the ham), images (of ham) in circulation, eyes in which one can see the attention to what is spoken or seen, and projectors which convey these signs of bodies to other eyes and ears (ham). (fix it)
